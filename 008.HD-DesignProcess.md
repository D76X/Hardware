# How to route SPI signal tracks.

Route SPI signals with short, direct traces, maintain consistent impedance, 
minimize crosstalk by spacing traces, avoid sharp angles, use a bus topology 
with dedicated chip select lines for each device, and place decoupling capacitors 
near the power pins of all SPI devices.

---

## Which resistor to use to pull down a bidirectional pin?

For a standard bidirectional pin, use a 10 kΩ resistor for general-purpose applications. 
If you need faster communication speeds on buses like I2C or SPI, use a 4.7 kΩ resistor.

Selecting the correct value requires balancing speed and power:

- General Purpose / Buttons (10 kΩ to 100 kΩ):  
Standard for pulling a pin to ground when idle. It limits the current when the pin is
driven high by an external device while keeping power consumption low.

- High-Speed Buses (1 kΩ to 4.7 kΩ):   
Faster edge transitions require a "stronger" pull-down to overcome trace capacitance. 
However, this increases current draw when the pin is actively high.

- Low Power / Battery Devices (100 kΩ to 1 MΩ):  
Minimizes current leakage but sacrifices switching speed because of a slower RC time constant.

### Key Considerations for Bidirectional Pins:

- Never use a resistor that is too low:  

A value like 100 will pull your line down fine but can damage the microcontroller or external 
device if the pin accidentally outputs a high signal.

- Internal pull-ups: 

Many microcontrollers (like STM32, Arduino, ESP32) have internal pull-up/pull-down resistors you 
can activate in your firmware, saving you from adding an external component.

-  Check your device's datasheet for internal resistance values (usually between 20k and 50k).

- Bus Communication:  

If this bidirectional pin is tied to an I2C or UART communication bus, verify if a pull-up resistor 
is required instead of a pull-down, as many communication protocols rely on an idle high state.

---

## Which resistor to use to pull up a bidirectional pin?

For most bidirectional pins, such as I2C lines or open-drain GPIOs, a 4.7k to 10k resistor 
is the standard choice. It balances power consumption with the speed needed for the signal 
to rise and fall cleanly.

Choosing the perfect resistor depends entirely on the requirements of your circuit:

1. General Rule of Thumb

- Standard 4.7k - 10k: 
The best all-rounder for breadboards and general microcontrollers.

- Low Power 10k - 100k: 
Best for battery-powered devices where you need to minimize current leakage.

2. The Core Technical Trade-off

Because a bidirectional pin must be able to "pull" the wire low to logic 0 as well as 
release it to go high to logic 1, you have to balance the resistor size.

- Too high e.g., 100k: 
The signal takes too long to rise, leading to communication errors or corrupted data 
at higher speeds.

- Too low 330:  
The pin has to "sink" too much current when pulling the line low, which can overheat or 
permanently damage the microcontroller.

3. Calculating the Exact Value

If you need a highly specific calculation, you must check your device's datasheet for 
two specific values:

- I_{OL} (Sink Current):   
The maximum current the pin can handle safely when pulling the line low (e.g., 2mA to 5mA for microcontrollers).

- Capacitance C_b:  
The physical length and capacitance of the wire/trace. Higher capacitance requires a stronger (lower resistance) 
pull-up. 

You apply Ohm's Law to determine the minimum safe resistor R based on your logic voltage V_cc:

`R = V_cc / I_OL`

> What is a Polyfuse - PPTC (Polymeric Positive Temperature Coefficient) ?

A polyfuse is a **resettable, self-healing circuit protection component used to prevent damage from overcurrent** faults in electronics. Unlike traditional, one-time fuses, polyfuses automatically reset to a low-resistance 
state once they cool down and the fault is removed.

## Block Diagram of the design

1. Power Supply: 

- the PCB needs stable power 
- use an AC to DC converter to bring down the mains voltage to a safe DC level
- the safe DC level feeds into the Power Supply Block (PSB) that distributes regulated voltage to the other parts of the PCB
- A Low Dropout (LDO) regulator compoment is used to supply with power all the sensitive components such as the Microcontroller, the RTC anD the switches.

---

2. AC to DC converter

- brings down the mains voltage to a safe DC level
- the safe DC level output of the AC-DC converter is used as input of a DC Power Block to generate a regulated DC voltage to distribute to the rest of the board 

---

3. LDO [Low-Dropout Regulator]

- the safe DC level output of the AC-DC converter is also fed into a LDO to generate at its output a
noise-free DC level used to power noise-sensitove components suche as RTC, switches, some FPGA, 
some Microcontrollers, etc.  

---
